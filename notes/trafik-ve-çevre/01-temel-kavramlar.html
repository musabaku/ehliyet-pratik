Temel Kavramlar — Core Definitions

Last updated: 2026-04-15
Slides covered: Slide 1 — Trafik, Karayolu, Araç, Taşıt; Slide 2 — Sürücü vs Şoför; Slide 3 — Trafik İşaretleri; Slide 4 — Geçiş Üstünlüğü vs Geçiş Hakkı; Slide 5 — Durma, Duraklama, Park Etme; Slide 6 — Trafik Kazası & Trafikten Men

1. Overview — Görev (Purpose)

Before you can answer a single Trafik ve Çevre question, you need to know exactly what these four words mean — Trafik, Karayolu, Araç, and Taşıt. They sound similar in Turkish but the exam deliberately swaps them in distractor answers. Getting these four definitions cold is the single cheapest win in the whole subject.

2. SLIDE 1 — The four base definitions

What the slide showed

A photo of a busy Istanbul highway (multi-lane urban road, cars, buses, pedestrians along the side) with four definitions underneath:

Trafik: Yayaların, hayvanların ve araçların karayolu üzerindeki hal ve hareketlerine TRAFİK denir.

Karayolu: Trafik için, kamunun yararlanmasına açık olan arazi şeridi, köprüler ve alanlardır.

Araç: Karayollarında kullanılabilen motorlu, motorsuz ve özel amaçlı taşıtlar ile iş makineleri ve lastik tekerlekli traktörlerin genel adıdır.

Taşıt: Karayolunda insan, hayvan ve yük taşımaya yarayan araçlardır. Bunlardan makine gücü ile yürütülenlere "Motorlu Taşıt", insan veya hayvan gücü ile yürütülenlere "Motorsuz Taşıt" denir.

Clean English explanation

Trafik (Traffic)

Traffic is the state and movement of pedestrians, animals, and vehicles on a road. Three things matter in the definition:
1. Who: yayalar (pedestrians), hayvanlar (animals), araçlar (vehicles) — all three are included.
2. Where: only on a karayolu (public road). A car moving in a private parking lot is not "trafik" in the legal sense.
3. What: both hal (state — e.g. standing still, parked, waiting) and hareket (movement). A parked car on the shoulder is also part of traffic.

A common exam trap: the student picks "only the movement of vehicles" — which is wrong on two counts (it excludes pedestrians/animals, and it excludes the stationary "hal" part).

Karayolu (Public Road)

A karayolu is a strip of land, bridges, and areas that is open to public use for traffic. The three parts that must appear in a correct definition:
1. Arazi şeridi — a strip of land
2. Köprüler — bridges (explicitly included)
3. Alanlar — areas / squares (meydanlar, public plazas)
4. Kamunun yararlanmasına açık — open to public use (the single most critical phrase)

Exam trap: if a question asks "is a private driveway a karayolu?" the answer is no — it's not open to the public. But a city square, a bridge over a river, and a highway shoulder all count.

Araç (Vehicle — the umbrella term)

"Araç" is the broadest umbrella term. It covers everything with wheels that can legally use a karayolu:
1. Motorlu taşıtlar — motor vehicles (cars, trucks, motorcycles, buses)
2. Motorsuz taşıtlar — non-motorized vehicles (bicycles, horse carts, handcarts)
3. Özel amaçlı taşıtlar — special-purpose vehicles (ambulances, fire trucks, police cars)
4. İş makineleri — work machines (excavators, loaders, forklifts, construction equipment)
5. Lastik tekerlekli traktörler — rubber-wheeled tractors

So every taşıt is an araç, but not every araç is a taşıt — iş makineleri and traktörler are araç but they are not taşıt in the strict sense because their primary job isn't carrying people/goods on a road.

Taşıt (Conveyance)

A taşıt is an araç used specifically for carrying people, animals, or goods on a karayolu. It is a subset of araç. Taşıt splits into two types based on power source:

Type Power source Examples
Motorlu taşıt Makine gücü (machine power) — engine Otomobil, kamyon, otobüs, motosiklet
Motorsuz taşıt İnsan veya hayvan gücü (human or animal power) Bisiklet, at arabası, el arabası

The mnemonic: motor in "motorlu" literally means engine. If there's no engine and it's pushed or pulled by a person or animal, it's motorsuz.

Critical exam fact

Trafik = hal + hareket (state AND movement), of yayalar + hayvanlar + araçlar, on a karayolu. If an answer option drops any one of these three groups, or drops "hal" and only says "hareket", it's wrong.

2B. SLIDE 2 — Sürücü vs Şoför (the person behind the wheel)

What the slide showed

Two photos side by side:
- Left: A man in a suit driving a private car (personal use).
- Right: A man in a taxi/minibus-driver uniform with cap, driving a commercial vehicle.

Below the photos, two definitions:

Sürücü: Karayolunda motorlu veya motorsuz bir aracı sevk ve idare eden kişidir.

Şoför: Karayolunda, ticari olarak tescil edilmiş bir motorlu taşıtı süren kişidir.

Clean English explanation

This is a classic two-name trap: two Turkish words that both translate to "driver" in English but have legally distinct meanings. The exam leans hard on the distinction.

Sürücü (Driver — general)

A sürücü is anyone who operates a vehicle on a public road, whether the vehicle is motorized or not. Three things matter:
1. Karayolu — must be on a public road (not a private driveway)
2. Motorlu veya motorsuz — either type of vehicle counts. A bicyclist is a sürücü. A horse-cart driver is a sürücü.
3. Sevk ve idare eden — "directs and manages" — i.e. actively controls the vehicle.

So: sürücü = anyone controlling any vehicle on a karayolu. The man in the suit driving his own car to work is a sürücü. So is the cyclist.

Şoför (Driver — professional)

A şoför is a much narrower term. Three tight conditions must all hold:
1. Karayolu — on a public road
2. Ticari olarak tescil edilmiş — the vehicle must be commercially registered (plate starts with certain codes, commercial insurance, yellow plate for taxis, etc.)
3. Motorlu taşıt — must be motorized AND must be a taşıt (so iş makineleri and non-motorized things are excluded)

A taxi driver, a city bus driver, a long-haul truck driver, a minibus (dolmuş) driver — these are all şoförler. A private citizen driving their own car to the grocery store is NOT a şoför, even though they are a sürücü.

Critical exam fact

Every şoför is a sürücü, but not every sürücü is a şoför.
Sürücü is the broad term (any vehicle, any driver). Şoför requires a commercially registered motor vehicle — professional/commercial driving only.

2C. The Sürücü ⊃ Şoför relationship

                       |
       ┌───────────────┼────────────────┐
       │               │                │
   bisikletli     özel otomobil       ŞOFÖR
   (cyclist)      sürücüsü       (ticari tescilli
                  (private car    motorlu taşıt süren)
                  driver)
                                  - taksi şoförü

The trap: the exam will show you two profiles and ask "which one is ONLY a sürücü and not a şoför?" → the private-car driver. Or "which is a şoför?" → the taxi driver. Read the plate color (yellow = commercial in Türkiye) or the phrase "ticari olarak tescil edilmiş" and you can decide instantly.

2D. SLIDE 3 — Trafik İşaretleri (Traffic Signs & Signals)

What the slide showed

Three pictures in a row:
- Left: A traffic police officer (trafik zabıtası) in a high-visibility jacket making a hand signal with his arm raised.
- Middle: A traffic light pole (kırmızı, sarı, yeşil).
- Right: A wooden box containing miniature versions of warning triangles, stop signs, parking signs, and other traffic signboards (işaret levhaları).

Definition underneath:

Trafik İşaretleri: Trafiği düzenleme amacı ile trafik zabıtası veya diğer yetkililerin yaptıkları işaretler ile ışıklı ve sesli işaretler, işaret levhaları ve yer işaretleridir.

Clean English explanation

"Trafik işaretleri" is the umbrella term for every officially recognized way of directing traffic on a road. The definition lists four distinct categories that all count as "trafik işaretleri" — the exam will quiz you on which categories are included.

The 4 categories of trafik işaretleri

  1. Trafik zabıtası / yetkililerin işaretleri — Hand signals made by traffic police or other authorized personnel. A police officer standing at an intersection waving you through is a "trafik işareti" in the legal sense.
  2. Işıklı işaretler — Light signals. Traffic lights (kırmızı/sarı/yeşil), flashing warning lights at level crossings, pedestrian signal heads.
  3. Sesli işaretler — Sound signals. Bells at railway crossings, audible pedestrian crossing beepers, siren-coordinated signals.
  4. İşaret levhaları — Sign boards. The whole library of metal signs: DUR, YOL VER, warning triangles, regulatory circles, informative rectangles, directional arrows.
  5. Yer işaretleri — Road markings. Lines painted on the road surface: lane lines, crosswalks (zebra), stop lines, arrows, parking bay lines.

(The slide text lists 4 items — "trafik zabıtası + ışıklı ve sesli işaretler + işaret levhaları + yer işaretleri" — but "ışıklı ve sesli" is frequently counted as two separate categories on the exam, giving 5 in total.)

The hierarchy of obedience (critical exam rule)

When these categories conflict with each other — e.g. a traffic light says GREEN but a police officer is waving STOP — there is a strict priority order you must follow. From highest to lowest:

  1. Trafik zabıtası / yetkili kişilerin işaretleri — hand signals from police or other authorized personnel (highest)
  2. Işıklı ve sesli işaretler — traffic lights and sound signals
  3. İşaret levhaları — sign boards (DUR, YOL VER, etc.)
  4. Yer işaretleri — road markings (lowest)

Mnemonic: "Polis > Işık > Levha > Yer". The human wins over the machine, the overhead signal wins over the roadside sign, and the roadside sign wins over the paint on the ground.

Critical exam fact

If the slide asks "what counts as a trafik işareti?" — all 4/5 categories count: polis işareti, ışıklı, sesli, işaret levhaları, yer işaretleri. If asked about precedence when two of them conflict: polis > ışıklı/sesli > levha > yer.

2E. SLIDE 4 — Geçiş Üstünlüğü vs Geçiş Hakkı (the classic two-name trap)

What the slide showed

Three images across the top:
- Left: An ambulance with flashing blue lights and siren (ambulans).
- Middle: A red fire truck (itfaiye).
- Right: The Turkish police emblem (polis).

Two definitions underneath:

Geçiş Üstünlüğü: Görev sırasında, belirli araç sürücülerinin can ve mal güvenliğini tehlikeye sokmamak şartı ile trafik kısıtlama veya yasaklarına bağlı olmamalarıdır.

Geçiş Hakkı: Yayaların ve araç kullananların diğer yaya ve araç kullananlara göre, yolu kullanma sırasındaki öncelik hakkıdır.

Clean English explanation

This is THE classic two-name trap of the Trafik ve Çevre exam — two terms that both translate loosely as "right of way" in English but are legally very different in Turkish. Expect at least 1–2 exam questions on this distinction. Getting them mixed up is the number one own-goal on this subject.

Geçiş Üstünlüğü (Priority passage — only for emergency vehicles)

Literally translates to "passage superiority." It is a special legal exemption granted to certain professional emergency vehicles while they are actively on duty with siren and flashing lights. These vehicles are not bound by normal traffic restrictions or prohibitions — they can:
- Exceed speed limits
- Drive through red lights
- Enter one-way streets against the flow
- Ignore no-entry and no-turn restrictions

But with one crucial condition: they must not endanger the life and property safety of others (can ve mal güvenliğini tehlikeye sokmamak şartı ile). If they cause an accident by using this privilege carelessly, they are liable.

Who has Geçiş Üstünlüğü?
- Ambulans (ambulance, 112)
- İtfaiye (fire department)
- Polis (police, 155)
- Jandarma (gendarmerie)
- Kan, organ nakli araçları (blood/organ transport vehicles)
- Cenaze araçları (funeral vehicles, in some cases)
- Sivil savunma araçları (civil defense)

Key triggers: siren ON + flashing lights ON + actively on a mission (görev sırasında). A parked ambulance or a police car driving normally without siren does NOT have geçiş üstünlüğü — they must obey traffic rules like anyone else.

Geçiş Hakkı (Right of way — for everyone)

Literally "passage right." This is the normal, everyday priority between two road users at a point where their paths could cross. It applies to all pedestrians and all drivers (not just emergency vehicles). It answers the question: "when two of us arrive at the same place, who goes first?"

Examples of geçiş hakkı rules:
- At an unsigned intersection, the vehicle coming from the right has geçiş hakkı.
- Pedestrians on a zebra crossing have geçiş hakkı over turning cars.
- A vehicle already on a roundabout has geçiş hakkı over a vehicle trying to enter.
- At a YOL VER (yield) sign, the main road traffic has geçiş hakkı.
- Rail vehicles (trams) generally have geçiş hakkı over road vehicles.

Critical exam fact

Geçiş ÜSTÜNLÜĞÜ = special exemption for emergency vehicles on duty (ambulans, itfaiye, polis) — allows them to ignore traffic rules as long as safety is not endangered.

Geçiş HAKKI = normal priority between everyday road users — who gets to go first at an intersection, zebra crossing, roundabout, etc.

The trap: in a question about a taxi vs a bus at an intersection, the answer is about geçiş hakkı. In a question about an ambulance running a red light, the answer is about geçiş üstünlüğü. Never swap them.

2F. The two-name trap side-by-side

Geçiş ÜSTÜNLÜĞÜ Geçiş HAKKI
Who has it Only emergency vehicles on duty (ambulans, itfaiye, polis, jandarma, …) Everyone — pedestrians and all drivers
When Görev sırasında (during the mission, siren + lights) Every time two road users meet
What it grants Exemption from traffic restrictions/prohibitions Priority order between equals
Bound by rules? No — can ignore red lights, speed limits, one-way, etc. Yes — decided by the rules

Mnemonic "Siren + lights = üstünlük" "Who goes first = hakkı"

2G. SLIDE 5 — Durma, Duraklama, Park Etme (the three-way stop trap)

What the slide showed

A top-down illustration of a car parallel-parking on a city street between two other parked cars, with green "safe distance" sensor arcs at the front and red "close distance" arcs at the rear — clearly evoking parking sensors. Below, three definitions:

Durma: Her türlü trafik zorunlulukları nedeni ile aracın durdurulmasıdır. (Kırmızı ışık, yol kapanması gibi)

Duraklama: Trafik zorunlulukları dışında araçların, insan indirmek-bindirmek, eşya yüklemek-boşaltmak veya beklemek amacı ile kısa bir süre için durdurulmasıdır.

Park Etme: Araçların durma ve duraklama halleri dışında, genelde uzun süreli bekletilmek üzere bırakılmasıdır.

Clean English explanation

Three words that all mean "stop" in English but have three completely different legal meanings in Turkish traffic law. This is a three-way trap (worse than the two-name traps) — the exam absolutely will test you on it.

The three are distinguished by two questions:
1. Why are you stopped? (Forced by traffic, or by your own choice?)
2. For how long? (Brief, or long-term?)

Durma (Stop — forced, involuntary)

You stopped because traffic forced you to. You had no choice. Examples:
- A red light is on (kırmızı ışık)
- The road ahead is closed (yol kapanması)
- A DUR sign at an intersection
- Police directing you to stop
- A pedestrian at a zebra crossing
- A traffic jam ahead

Key phrase: "Trafik zorunlulukları nedeni ile" — because of traffic obligations. The driver didn't want to stop; the traffic system required it.

Duration: as long as the obligation lasts — could be 10 seconds (red light) or 10 minutes (jam). Duration is not the defining factor. The reason is.

Duraklama (Brief stop — voluntary but short)

You chose to stop for a short time, for one of exactly three reasons:
1. İnsan indirmek / bindirmek — to drop off / pick up a person
2. Eşya yüklemek / boşaltmak — to load / unload goods
3. Beklemek — to briefly wait (kısa bir süre)

Key phrases: "Trafik zorunlulukları dışında" (outside of traffic obligations — you chose this) + "kısa bir süre için" (for a short duration).

Duration: short — the slide doesn't pin a number, but by convention on the exam, "kısa süre" means minutes, not hours. If you get out of the car and walk away, it stops being duraklama.

Example: Pulling over in front of a friend's house to let them out of the car. The car is running, you're waiting at the wheel, they grab their bag and go. That's duraklama.

Park Etme (Parking — voluntary and long-term)

You stopped for a long time and left the vehicle in that state. Key phrase: "uzun süreli bekletilmek üzere bırakılmasıdır" — left to wait for a long time. The driver typically turns off the engine, exits, and walks away.

Examples:
- Leaving your car in a parking lot while you shop
- Parking in front of your home overnight
- Parking at the office for the workday

The decision tree

Are you stopping because traffic FORCED you?
├── YES → DURMA (red light, DUR sign, jam)
└── NO → you chose it
        │
        How long will you be stopped?
        ├── Short, for drop-off/load/brief wait → DURAKLAMA
        └── Long, you left the vehicle        → PARK ETME

Critical exam fact

Durma = forced by traffic (kırmızı ışık, DUR). Duraklama = short voluntary stop (indir-bindir, yükle-boşalt, kısa bekleme). Park Etme = long-term, you left the vehicle.

The exam will describe a scenario and ask which one it is. Decode it by: (1) was it required or chosen? — if required, it's durma. (2) if chosen, short or long? — short is duraklama, long is park etme.

2H. SLIDE 6 — Trafik Kazası & Trafikten Men (accident + removal from traffic)

What the slide showed

An aerial photo of a real traffic accident scene: a minibus overturned on its side at a roundabout, with a large crowd of bystanders gathered around it and other cars scattered nearby. Two definitions underneath:

Trafik Kazası: Karayolu üzerinde hareket halinde olan bir veya birden fazla aracın karıştığı, ölüm, yaralanma veya zararla sonuçlanmış olaydır.

Trafikten Men: Trafik zabıtasınca, kanunda belirtilen hallerde araç ile ilgili belgelerin alınması ve aracın belirli bir yere çekilerek trafikten alıkonulmasıdır.

Clean English explanation

Trafik Kazası (Traffic accident)

For an event to legally count as a "trafik kazası," all four of the following must be true:
1. Karayolu üzerinde — it happened on a public road (not on a private driveway, parking lot, construction site, etc.)
2. Hareket halinde — at least one vehicle was in motion at the moment of the incident (a parked car being scratched by a pedestrian is not a trafik kazası in the strict sense)
3. Bir veya birden fazla araç — at least one vehicle was involved (one-car crashes like hitting a wall still count; so do multi-car pileups)
4. Ölüm, yaralanma veya zararla sonuçlanmış — it resulted in death, injury, OR material damage. Any ONE of these three outcomes is enough.

The trap: the exam may describe a scenario — e.g. a child is hit in a public parking lot — and ask "is this a trafik kazası?" Strictly, a public parking lot may or may not be a "karayolu" depending on whether it's open to public use. Pay attention to the location word in the question.

Another trap: if a car is parked and another car scrapes it while driving by, yes, it's still a trafik kazası — because one of the vehicles was in motion. The definition only requires that at least one vehicle be "hareket halinde."

Trafikten Men (Removal from traffic)

A legal/administrative action performed by the traffic police (trafik zabıtası), not the driver. When the law specifies certain violations (kanunda belirtilen haller), the police can:
1. Seize the vehicle's documents (belgelerin alınması) — registration, license plate, insurance, etc.
2. Tow the vehicle to a designated location (belirli bir yere çekilerek)
3. Ban it from further use in traffic (trafikten alıkonulmasıdır)

This is NOT the same as being pulled over or fined. Trafikten men means the vehicle itself is removed from circulation — you physically cannot drive it until the issue is resolved. Typical triggers:
- No valid registration / expired inspection (muayene)
- No valid insurance (sigorta)
- Driving under the influence (alcohol/drugs) beyond certain limits
- Unroadworthy condition (broken lights, worn tires, no brakes)
- Driving without a license, or with a fake/suspended license

Key distinction: a ceza (fine) punishes the driver. A trafikten men takes the vehicle off the road.

Critical exam fact

Trafik Kazası = 4 conditions: karayolunda + en az bir araç hareket halinde + araç karışmış + ölüm/yaralanma/zarar sonucu. Miss any one → not a trafik kazası.

Trafikten Men = police physically removing the vehicle from traffic (seizing its documents and towing it to a designated place). It's a legal penalty applied to the vehicle, not the driver.

3. The containment hierarchy (must know cold)

                        ARAÇ  (umbrella — everything legally on the road)
                       /     \
                      /       \
                  TAŞIT        NON-taşıt araç
                (carries        (iş makinesi,
                 people/         traktör)
                 goods)
                   |
        ┌──────────┴──────────┐
        │                     │
    MOTORLU              MOTORSUZ
    (engine)             (insan/hayvan)
    - otomobil           - bisiklet
    - kamyon             - at arabası
    - otobüs             - el arabası
    - motosiklet

Why this matters: the exam will ask "which of the following is a taşıt but not a motorlu taşıt?" → bisiklet. Or "which is an araç but not a taşıt?" → iş makinesi or traktör. You cannot answer these without the hierarchy.

4. Why this matters for the exam

  1. Q: "Trafiğin tanımı hangisidir?" (What is the definition of traffic?)
    → The answer must include yaya + hayvan + araç AND hal + hareket AND karayolu. Distractors drop one element.

  2. Q: "Aşağıdakilerden hangisi motorsuz taşıttır?" (Which of the following is a motorless conveyance?)
    → Bisiklet, at arabası, el arabası. Distractor: "motosiklet" (has "motor" in the name → it's motorlu).

  3. Q: "Aşağıdakilerden hangisi karayolu tanımına girer?" (Which fits the road definition?)
    → Bridges (köprüler) and public squares (alanlar) — yes. Private driveway — no.

  4. Q: "Araç ile taşıt arasındaki fark nedir?" (What's the difference between araç and taşıt?)
    → Araç is the broader term. Every taşıt is an araç, but iş makineleri and traktörler are araç but not taşıt.

  5. Q: "İnsan gücü ile yürütülen taşıta ne denir?" (What's a human-powered conveyance called?)
    → Motorsuz taşıt.

  6. Q: "Sürücü ile şoför arasındaki fark nedir?" (Difference between sürücü and şoför?)
    → Sürücü = anyone operating any vehicle (motorlu or motorsuz). Şoför = driver of a commercially registered motor vehicle. Every şoför is a sürücü; not every sürücü is a şoför.

  7. Q: "Bisikletini süren kişi nedir?" (What is a person riding a bicycle?)
    → Sürücü. Not a şoför (bicycle has no engine + not commercially registered).

  8. Q: "Taksiyi kullanan kişiye ne denir?" (What do you call a person driving a taxi?)
    → Şoför (also correctly a sürücü — but şoför is the more precise answer because the taxi is ticari olarak tescil edilmiş).

  9. Q: "Trafik işaretleri nelerdir?" (What are traffic signs?)
    → The correct answer lists all of: trafik zabıtası işaretleri + ışıklı/sesli işaretler + işaret levhaları + yer işaretleri. Distractors drop one or two categories.

  10. Q: "Trafik işaretlerinin uyulma sırası nedir?" (What is the order of obedience for traffic signs?)
    → Trafik zabıtası > Işıklı/sesli > İşaret levhaları > Yer işaretleri. The human always wins over the machine.

  11. Q: "Kırmızı ışık yanıyor ama trafik polisi geç diye işaret ediyor. Ne yapmalısın?" (Red light is on but the traffic cop is waving you through. What do you do?)
    → Obey the polis. Trafik zabıtası has the highest priority in the sign hierarchy.

  12. Q: "Geçiş üstünlüğüne sahip araçlar hangileridir?" (Which vehicles have geçiş üstünlüğü?)
    → Ambulans, itfaiye, polis, jandarma, kan/organ nakli araçları, sivil savunma. Only when on duty, with siren + flashing lights.

  13. Q: "Geçiş üstünlüğü hangi şartla kullanılabilir?" (Under what condition can geçiş üstünlüğü be used?)
    → Can ve mal güvenliğini tehlikeye sokmamak şartı ile — without endangering life or property.

  14. Q: "Bir ambulans sirenleri açık şekilde kırmızı ışıkta geçebilir mi?" (Can an ambulance pass a red light with siren on?)
    → Yes — it has geçiş üstünlüğü. But not if doing so would endanger anyone.

  15. Q: "Bir kavşakta iki otomobil karşılaştığında hangisine denir?" (When two cars meet at an intersection, what concept applies?)
    → Geçiş hakkı (right of way between normal road users) — NOT geçiş üstünlüğü.

  16. Q: "Aşağıdakilerden hangisi geçiş üstünlüğüne sahiptir?" (Which of the following has geçiş üstünlüğü?)
    → Ambulans / itfaiye / polis — never a taxi, a school bus, a public bus, a municipal vehicle, or a dolmuş.

  17. Q: "Kırmızı ışıkta durmak nedir?" (Stopping at a red light is what?)
    → Durma — it is forced by traffic obligations (trafik zorunlulukları nedeni ile).

  18. Q: "İnsan indirmek-bindirmek için kısa süre durmak nedir?" (Briefly stopping to drop off / pick up a passenger is what?)
    → Duraklama. Short, voluntary, for one of the three reasons (indir-bindir, yükle-boşalt, kısa bekleme).

  19. Q: "Park etmenin tanımı nedir?" (What is parking?)
    → Stopping the vehicle for a long time, outside of durma/duraklama — typically with the driver leaving it behind.

  20. Q: "Durma, duraklama, park etme farkı nedir?" (Difference between the three?)
    → Durma = forced (traffic). Duraklama = chosen + short. Park etme = chosen + long.

  21. Q: "Trafik kazası tanımı hangi unsurları içerir?" (What elements define a traffic accident?)
    → 4 elements: karayolunda + en az bir aracın hareket halinde olması + araç karışması + ölüm/yaralanma/zarar ile sonuçlanması.

  22. Q: "Trafikten men nedir?" (What is removal from traffic?)
    → The traffic police seize the vehicle's documents and tow it to a designated location, taking the vehicle off the road. A penalty applied to the vehicle, not the driver.

  23. Q: "Trafikten men kim tarafından yapılır?" (Who carries out trafikten men?)
    → Trafik zabıtası (traffic police). Not the driver, not a regular ceza notice, not the court.

5. Memorize These

TRAFİK = Yayaların, hayvanların ve araçların karayolu üzerindeki hal ve hareketleri.
Traffic is the state and movement of pedestrians, animals, and vehicles on a public road.

KARAYOLU = Trafik için, kamunun yararlanmasına açık olan arazi şeridi, köprüler ve alanlar.
A public road is a strip of land, bridges, and areas open to public use for traffic.

ARAÇ = Karayolunda kullanılabilen motorlu, motorsuz, özel amaçlı taşıtlar + iş makineleri + lastik tekerlekli traktörlerin genel adı.
"Araç" is the umbrella name for every wheeled thing legally on the road.

TAŞIT = Karayolunda insan, hayvan ve yük taşımaya yarayan araçlar.
Makine gücü → Motorlu Taşıt. İnsan/hayvan gücü → Motorsuz Taşıt.

SÜRÜCÜ = Karayolunda motorlu veya motorsuz bir aracı sevk ve idare eden kişi.
Anyone operating any vehicle on a public road — including cyclists and horse-cart drivers.

ŞOFÖR = Karayolunda ticari olarak tescil edilmiş bir motorlu taşıtı süren kişi.
The driver of a commercially registered motor vehicle — taxi, bus, truck, dolmuş.

Every şoför is a sürücü, but not every sürücü is a şoför.
