1. Overview — Neden bu konu?

This topic is from the portal's "Güvenli Araç Kullanma, Araç Bakım ve Kontrolleri" subject (Araç Tekniği subject 2/6). It covers everything a driver should do before, during, and around driving to stay safe and keep the car in good shape. The first block is araç kullanmaya hazırlık — the routine you should follow every time you approach your car.

Three things matter on the exam here:

  1. Pre-drive (araç dışı + araç içi) checks — what to look at before you even sit down
  2. Correct seating / driving position — seat back angle, body contact, arm reach
  3. Adjustment of mirrors, head restraint, seatbelt — cabin setup

The underlying principle: "Doğru hazırlık = kısa reaksiyon süresi + maksimum kontrol" — correct preparation gives you the shortest possible reaction time and the best control of the car.

2. SLIDE 1 — Araca Binmeden Önce Ne Yapılır? (Pre-drive External Check)

What the slide showed

Verbatim Turkish → English

ARACA BİNMEDEN ÖNCE NE YAPILIR.?
Lastiklerin göz kontrolü, fren lambalarını, stop lambaları, sinyaller, benzin depo kapağına, ve yağ akıntısına bakılmalıdır. Aracın altında herhangi bir şey olup, kedi ve köpeğin olmadığının kontrolü yapılır.

WHAT IS DONE BEFORE GETTING INTO THE CAR?
Visual inspection of the tires, brake lights, stop lamps, signals (turn indicators), the fuel tank cap, and any oil leaks should be checked. A check is also made for anything under the car, including cats and dogs.

The 7-item pre-drive walk-around

This is a memorize-the-list question on the exam. The 7 items to check before you enter the car:

# Turkish English Why it matters
1 Lastiklerin göz kontrolü Visual tire check Low air pressure, cuts, bulges, or nails in the tread are all immediate safety risks. Look at all 4 tires — are they properly inflated? Any visible damage?
2 Fren lambaları Brake lights Must light up when you press the pedal. If one is burned out, you're invisible to the car behind you when braking — huge rear-end collision risk.
3 Stop lambaları Stop lamps (same as fren lambaları, redundant wording) In Turkish driving curriculum, "stop lambası" and "fren lambası" are used interchangeably to refer to the red rear brake light.
4 Sinyaller Turn signals / indicators Front and rear signal lamps must flash when activated. Broken signals = illegal + dangerous.
5 Benzin depo kapağı Fuel tank cap Must be securely closed. A loose fuel cap lets vapor escape, triggers the check-engine light, and in rare cases can leak fuel.
6 Yağ akıntısı Oil leaks (on the ground under the car) A dark puddle under the engine bay means oil is leaking → stop driving, because low oil → engine damage.
7 Aracın altında herhangi bir şey + kedi ve köpek kontrolü Check under the car for any object / cats and dogs Classic Turkish driving-culture item: cats especially love to sleep under parked cars for warmth. Always check before starting the engine. Also check for rocks, branches, parking blocks, or children's toys.

The "kedi ve köpek" item — why it's always on Turkish exams

Turkish driving curriculum takes this very seriously — partly because stray cats are extremely common in Turkish neighborhoods, and partly because it's an easily memorizable exam question. Hoca will hammer this point:

Aracı çalıştırmadan önce aracın altını kontrol et — özellikle kış aylarında kediler motor altında uyur.
Before starting the car, check underneath — especially in winter, cats sleep under the engine.

Exam trigger phrase: "Araca binmeden önce aracın altı neden kontrol edilir?" → Kedi, köpek veya herhangi bir engelin olmadığının görülmesi için.

Critical exam fact

Araca binmeden önce lastikler, fren/stop lambaları, sinyaller, benzin deposu kapağı, yağ akıntısı ve aracın altı (kedi-köpek dahil) kontrol edilir.
Before getting into the car, check tires, brake/stop lamps, signals, fuel cap, oil leaks, and underneath the car (including for cats and dogs).

4. SLIDE 3 — Doğru Oturma Pozisyonu (Correct Seating Position — Rules)

What the slide showed

A full-slide text paragraph (blue text) explaining the four rules for correct driving posture: seat back angle, shoulder contact, left-foot leverage, arm reach to the steering wheel, plus the consequences of a wrong position.

Verbatim Turkish → English

Koltuğa oturduğunuzda koltuğa sırt ayarı yaklaşık 90 derecelik bir açıda olmalıdır. Omuzlarınızın koltuk sırtıyla sıkı bağlantısı olmalı, sol ayaktan kuvvet alarak vücudun koltuk sırtlığına tamamen yapışması gerekir. Bu pozisyon alındıktan sonra ellerin direksiyon simidinin üst bölümünü kolaylıkla kavrayabileceği kadar koltuk ileri alınmalıdır. Aksi taktirde direksiyondan uzak kalınır. Bu pozisyon vücudun rahat hareket etmesine ve reaksiyon süresinin daha uzun sürmesine sebep olur, sert manevralarda da öne savrulmayı önler.

When you sit in the seat, the seat-back angle should be approximately 90 degrees. Your shoulders must be firmly in contact with the seat back, and by pushing with your left foot, your body should fully adhere to the seat back. Once this position is set, the seat should be moved forward just enough that your hands can easily grip the top section of the steering wheel. Otherwise you'll be too far from the wheel. This position allows the body to move comfortably and [extends reaction time — SLIDE TYPO, see note below], and also prevents being thrown forward during hard maneuvers.

The 4 rules — broken down

Rule 1 — Seat back angle: ~90 degrees

Koltuğa oturduğunuzda koltuğa sırt ayarı yaklaşık 90 derecelik bir açıda olmalıdır.
When seated, the seat-back angle should be approximately 90°.

Plain English: The backrest should be nearly upright — close to a right angle with the seat cushion, not reclined. A reclined "lounging" position looks comfortable but it's dangerous:

  • You can't see the road clearly over the dashboard
  • Your shoulders pull away from the seat → less control, delayed reaction
  • The seatbelt doesn't sit correctly across your chest
  • In a crash, you can "submarine" under the seatbelt (slide forward/under it) and suffer severe internal injuries

Exam-ready phrasing: "Sürücü koltuğunun sırt açısı yaklaşık 90° olmalıdır."

Rule 2 — Shoulders must stay in contact with the seat back

Omuzlarınızın koltuk sırtıyla sıkı bağlantısı olmalı.
Your shoulders must be in firm contact with the seat back.

Plain English: When you turn the steering wheel hard (e.g., sharp corner, emergency swerve), your body naturally wants to rotate forward away from the seat. If your shoulders leave the seat back, you lose leverage — you're essentially fighting the steering wheel with your arms alone, not your core body. By keeping both shoulder blades pressed against the seat back, you can use your whole torso for strength and accuracy.

Exam angle: "Sürüş esnasında omuzlar koltuk sırtından ayrılmamalıdır."

Rule 3 — "Sol ayaktan kuvvet alarak" — the left-foot brace

Sol ayaktan kuvvet alarak vücudun koltuk sırtlığına tamamen yapışması gerekir.
By taking force from the left foot, the body should fully press against the seat back.

Plain English: Most people don't realize this, but in a manual transmission car, when your sol ayak (left foot) is not pressing the clutch, it should be braced firmly against the floor (or against the dedicated foot-rest / dead-pedal / "ayak dayama yeri") on the far left. This brace does two critical things:

  1. Pushes your body back into the seat — your legs straighten slightly, which pushes your hips and back against the seat back, locking you in place
  2. Stabilizes your body during hard cornering and braking — without the brace, sharp turns throw your upper body sideways and you lose control of the steering

In an automatic car, the same principle applies — the dead pedal on the far left exists specifically so the left foot can brace.

Exam angle: this is the reason the answer to "Ayaklar nasıl konumlandırılmalıdır?" includes the left-foot brace — not just "ayaklar rahat dursun".

Rule 4 — Arm reach: fingertips to the top of the steering wheel

Ellerin direksiyon simidinin üst bölümünü kolaylıkla kavrayabileceği kadar koltuk ileri alınmalıdır. Aksi taktirde direksiyondan uzak kalınır.
The seat should be moved forward just enough that your hands can easily grip the top section of the steering wheel. Otherwise you'll end up too far from the wheel.

Plain English: With your shoulders firmly against the seat back (rule 2), extend one arm forward — your wrist should rest comfortably on the top of the steering wheel. That's the reference point. If you can't reach it without leaning forward, you're too far back — move the seat forward. If your arm is folded sharply with elbows against your body, you're too close — move it back.

Why this arm-length rule works:
- At this distance, when you grip the wheel normally (at 9 and 3, or 10 and 2), your elbows are slightly bent — not locked straight, not cramped tight
- Slightly bent elbows give you maximum range of motion for steering corrections
- You have the leverage to turn the wheel fully lock-to-lock without losing shoulder contact with the seat

Exam trigger phrase: "Direksiyon simidinin üst noktasına kol uzatıldığında bilek direksiyonun üzerine gelmelidir."

⚠️ Note about the slide's reaction-time wording

The slide says:

"Bu pozisyon vücudun rahat hareket etmesine ve reaksiyon süresinin daha uzun sürmesine sebep olur…"

Taken literally, this says "the correct position causes longer reaction time" — which is the opposite of the truth. Correct posture gives shorter (faster) reaction time, not longer. This appears to be a typo in the slide (most likely meant to say daha kısa sürmesine — "shorter").

What you should remember for the exam:
- Doğru oturma pozisyonu = reaksiyon süresini KISALTIR (shortens) — not lengthens
- This is the standard Turkish exam answer

If you see a question like "Doğru sürüş pozisyonunun faydası nedir?" the correct answer is always that it reduces reaction time and prevents forward throw (öne savrulma) in hard maneuvers. Ignore the typo on the slide.

The 4 benefits of correct seating position

# Turkish English
1 Vücudun rahat hareket etmesini sağlar Allows the body to move comfortably
2 Reaksiyon süresini kısaltır (NOT uzatır — see typo note) Shortens reaction time
3 Sert manevralarda öne savrulmayı önler Prevents being thrown forward during hard maneuvers
4 Direksiyondan uzak kalmayı önler Prevents sitting too far from the steering wheel

Critical exam facts

Sürüş pozisyonu: sırt 90°, omuzlar koltuğa yapışık, sol ayak payandada, kol uzatıldığında bilek direksiyon üstünde.
Driving position: back 90°, shoulders against the seat, left foot on the dead pedal, wrist resting on top of the steering wheel when the arm is extended.

Doğru sürüş pozisyonu reaksiyon süresini kısaltır ve sert manevralarda öne savrulmayı önler.
Correct driving position shortens reaction time and prevents forward-throw in hard maneuvers.

4B. SLIDE 4 — Direksiyon Tutuş Pozisyonu: 9:15 + Sürüş Pozisyonu Diyagramı

What the slide showed

Top of the slide: a paragraph in blue text explaining why seating position alone is not enough — the steering wheel grip must also be correct. Then two images:

  1. Left: a photograph of a man sitting in a driving simulator rig, with 4 numbered yellow labels pointing to the key contact points of the body with the car: 1 = feet on pedals, 2 = hips/back against the seat, 3 = arms on the steering wheel, 4 = head against the headrest.
  2. Right: a simple stick-figure diagram labeled "SÜRÜŞ POZİSYONU" (driving position), showing a driver with an angle marker of "90° à 120° MAX" at the elbow — meaning the arms should be bent to somewhere between 90° and 120° (not straight, not tightly folded).

Verbatim Turkish → English

Ancak her şey oturma pozisyonuyla bitmiyor. Direksiyon simidinin tutulmasıyla ilgili sorunu da çözmemiz gerekiyor. Sol el direksiyonda, sağ elle vitesle arkadaşlık ediyorsa ya da 10:15 veya 10:10 pozisyonunu doğru zannediyorsanız yanılıyorsunuz. Direksiyon simidi mutlaka 9:15 pozisyonunda tutulmalıdır. Bu durumda her iki yöne de maksimum hareket kabiliyeti elde edilir.

But it doesn't all end with the seating position. We also have to solve the problem of how to hold the steering wheel. If your left hand is on the wheel and your right hand is keeping the gear lever company, or if you think 10:15 or 10:10 is the correct position, you're wrong. The steering wheel must be held in the 9:15 position. In this position, you get the maximum range of motion in both directions.

The 9:15 rule — explained

The 9:15 position refers to the positions of the hands on the steering wheel, imagined as the hands of a clock:

  • Left hand → 9 o'clock (horizontally to the left, exactly at the equator of the wheel)
  • Right hand → 3 o'clock (horizontally to the right, exactly opposite the left hand)

In Turkish clock-time, 9:15 means the big hand at 3 (the "15 minutes past" position) and the little hand at 9 — so the slide's "9:15" is shorthand for "both hands at the 9 and 3 positions" (which in English-speaking driving schools is sometimes called "9 and 3").

Why 9:15 is correct (and 10:10 / 10:15 is wrong)

The older driving-school standard was 10 and 2 (10:10) — hands near the top of the wheel. Modern driving instruction has moved to 9 and 3 (9:15) for several reasons:

  1. Maximum turning range in both directions. From 9 and 3, you can turn the wheel a full 180° in either direction without having to cross your hands or let go. From 10 and 2, your range is shorter before your arms lock up.
  2. Airbag safety. In a crash, the steering wheel airbag deploys explosively from the center of the wheel. If your hands are at 10 and 2, the airbag can slam your hands and arms into your own face. At 9 and 3, your hands are out of the airbag's direct path.
  3. Better leverage and control. At 9 and 3, your arms are in their strongest pushing/pulling position, and your shoulders stay anchored to the seat back (rule 2 from slide 3).
  4. Symmetry. You have equal reach and leverage in both directions — important for quick emergency corrections.

The wrong positions the slide warns about

Wrong position What it looks like Why it's wrong
Sol el direksiyonda, sağ elle vites Only the left hand on the wheel; the right hand is resting on the gear stick You have only one hand on the wheel → half the control, half the leverage, none of the symmetry. The right hand should be on the wheel except during the brief moment of a gear change.
10:10 (iki el üstte) Both hands near the top of the wheel Too high → crosses into airbag deployment zone, less range of motion, elbows too high for good leverage
10:15 Left at 10, right at 3 (asymmetric) Asymmetric grip → unequal strength in each direction

The two support images

Image 1 — The 4 body contact points (numbered photo)

The simulator-rig photo shows the four physical anchor points of a correctly-seated driver:

# Body part Contact point Why it matters
1 Ayaklar (feet) On the pedals + left foot braced on dead pedal Controls gas/brake/clutch; left foot braces the body
2 Kalça ve sırt (hips and back) Pressed into the seat cushion and seat back Anchors the body, keeps shoulders on the seat back
3 Kollar (arms) Gripping the steering wheel at 9:15 Controls direction; elbows slightly bent for range
4 Baş (head) Against the koltuk başlığı / başlık (headrest) Protects the neck in rear-end impacts (prevents whiplash)

Memorize this as the 4 contact points: ayaklar → kalça/sırt → kollar → baş. If any one of these is not in correct contact, the posture is wrong.

Image 2 — The "90° – 120° MAX" stick figure

The stick-figure diagram labels the elbow angle as 90° to 120° maximum. This is the ideal arm bend when gripping the steering wheel at 9:15:

  • Less than 90° (arms tightly folded) → seat is too close → you can't turn the wheel fully and your chest is too close to the airbag
  • More than 120° (arms nearly straight) → seat is too far back → you lose leverage and shoulders come off the seat back
  • Between 90° and 120° → optimal — enough leverage for emergency maneuvers, enough range of motion to turn the wheel lock-to-lock, elbows still slightly bent so you can push and pull the wheel freely

This is why slide 3 rule 4 works: when you extend your arm straight forward from a correct posture, your wrist rests on the top of the wheel — and when you then lower your hand to the 9 o'clock position, your elbow naturally ends up bent at roughly 90°–120°.

During this slide, hoca paused to draw a rough clock face on the slide by hand, in brown pen with red X marks. The drawing shows:

  • A rough circular clock face with the clock numbers sketched around it (12 at top, 3 at right, 6 at bottom, 9 at left)
  • A red X at the 12 o'clock position (to show that's NOT where hands go)

  • Sol el (left hand) → 9 konumunda (at the 9 o'clock position, horizontally to the left)

Critical exam fact

Direksiyon simidi 9:15 pozisyonunda tutulmalıdır. Kollar 90° – 120° arasında bükülü olmalıdır.
The steering wheel must be held in the 9:15 position. Arms should be bent between 90° and 120°.

9:15 = sol el saatin 9'u, sağ el saatin 3'ü konumunda.

Doğru sürüş pozisyonunun 4 temas noktası: ayaklar, kalça/sırt, kollar/direksiyon, baş/başlık.

4C. SLIDE 5 — Araca Binildiğinde Yapılması Gereken Hususlar (Interior / Pre-Start Checks)

What the slide showed

Title: ARACA BİNİLDİĞİNDE YAPILMASI GEREKEN HUSUSLAR (Things to do once you've gotten into the car). A checklist of 4 interior items the driver must set up before starting the engine. This is the inside-the-car counterpart to slide 1 (which was the outside walk-around).

Verbatim Turkish → English

ARACA BİNİLDİĞİNDE YAPILMASI GEREKEN HUSUSLAR
Araca binen sürücü, motoru çalıştırmadan önce, şu hususları yerine getirmelidir:

  • Sürücü Koltuğu: Sürücü, koltuğunu aracı rahatça kullanabilecek şekilde kendine uygun biçimde ayarlamalıdır.
  • İç ve Dış Aynaların Durumu: Aracın iç ve dış aynalarını, trafiği rahat bir biçimde gözetleyebilecek şekilde ayarlamalıdır.
  • Emniyet Kemerinin Kontrolü: Emniyet kemeri sürücüye en uygun biçimde ayarlanarak, araçta bulunan yolcuya da takılmalıdır.
  • Sürücü Mahalli Kontrolü: Sürücü, kullanacağı aydınlatma sistemi anahtarlarını, aksesuarları (radyo, fan vb.) ve el freninin yerini kontrol etmelidir.

Things to do after getting into the car
Before starting the engine, the driver must:

  • Driver's seat: The driver should adjust the seat to the position that allows comfortable operation of the vehicle.
  • Interior and exterior mirrors: Adjust the inside (rearview) and outside mirrors so traffic can be easily observed.
  • Seatbelt check: The seatbelt should be adjusted properly for the driver, and should also be fastened by any passengers in the car.
  • Driver compartment check: The driver should locate and check the lighting-system switches (headlights, park, fog, signals), the accessories (radio, fan, AC, etc.), and the location of the handbrake.

The 4 interior setup items — explained

Item 1 — Sürücü koltuğu ayarı (Driver's seat)

Plain English: This ties directly to slides 3 and 4 — before you start the engine, set the seat so that:
- The seat-back angle is ~90° (slide 3, rule 1)
- Your shoulders stay against the seat back (slide 3, rule 2)
- Your left foot braces on the dead pedal (slide 3, rule 3)
- Your wrist rests on the top of the steering wheel when you extend your arm (slide 3, rule 4)
- Your elbows are bent 90°–120° when gripping at 9:15 (slide 4)

If you can't achieve all of these, the seat is not in the right position — adjust before starting the engine, not after.

Item 2 — İç ve dış aynaların ayarı (Interior and exterior mirrors)

Plain English: The three mirrors in a passenger car:

Turkish English Position
İç ayna Interior rearview mirror On the windshield, center — shows straight behind through the rear window
Sol dış ayna Left exterior mirror Left side of the car — shows the left rear and blind spot
Sağ dış ayna Right exterior mirror Right side of the car — shows the right rear and blind spot

How to adjust them correctly:
- İç ayna: You should be able to see the entire rear window framed in the mirror. Head position: in your normal driving posture (don't lean).
- Dış aynalar: Adjust them so you see a small sliver of your own car's side at the inner edge of the mirror, and as much of the adjacent lane as possible on the outer edge. This minimizes the kör nokta (blind spot — see slide 6).

Exam trigger phrase: "Aynalar motor çalıştırılmadan önce ayarlanır" — mirror adjustment happens before starting the engine, not while driving.

Item 3 — Emniyet kemerinin kontrolü (Seatbelt check)

Plain English: The driver's own seatbelt must be properly fastened, adjusted, and positioned — and every passenger in the car (front and rear) must also be wearing one. The slide makes this explicit: "araçta bulunan yolcuya da takılmalıdır" — the seatbelt must also be fastened by passengers in the car.

Why the slide emphasizes passengers: Turkish law makes the driver legally responsible for ensuring all passengers wear their seatbelts, including rear-seat passengers. If a passenger is unbelted and you're stopped or in an accident, the driver receives the fine.

Classic exam angle:
- "Emniyet kemerini kim takmakla yükümlüdür?" → Sürücü ve tüm yolcular (driver and all passengers).
- "Arka koltuktaki yolcu emniyet kemeri takmak zorunda mıdır?" → Evet (yes).
- "Emniyet kemeri takmayan yolcunun cezasını kim öder?" → Sürücü (the driver).

Seatbelt is a pasif güvenlik sistemi (passive safety system — see 09-fren-sistemi.md). It only works if worn correctly: across the chest (not under the arm), across the hips (not on the stomach), and snugly (no slack).

Item 4 — Sürücü mahalli kontrolü (Driver compartment check)

Plain English: Before starting the engine, the driver should locate and familiarize themselves with the controls they'll need while driving — especially in a car they don't drive regularly (rental, borrowed, different model):

  • Aydınlatma sistemi anahtarları → headlight switch, park light switch, fog light switch, high-beam, signal lever. You should know without looking how to turn each one on.
  • Aksesuarlar → radio, fan, AC, defroster, wipers, washer fluid control.
  • El freni → Where is it? Is it a lever between the seats? A foot-pedal handbrake? An electronic button? Find it before you need it. On a hill, you don't want to fumble for the handbrake location.

Why this matters: the time to learn where things are is not when you're rolling backward down a hill or trying to turn on the fog lights in sudden rain. Do it before the engine starts.

Critical exam fact

Araca binildikten sonra motoru çalıştırmadan önce: koltuk ayarı, ayna ayarı, emniyet kemeri (tüm yolcular dahil), sürücü mahalli kontrolü yapılır.
After getting in the car but before starting the engine: seat adjustment, mirror adjustment, seatbelt (including all passengers), driver compartment check.

Sequence recap: the 3 phases of "hazırlık"

Phase When What
Phase 1 — Araç dışı kontrol Before getting in Tires, lamps, signals, fuel cap, oil leaks, under the car (slide 1)
Phase 2 — Araç içi ayar After getting in, engine OFF Seat, mirrors, seatbelt, compartment controls (slide 5)
Phase 3 — Aracı çalıştırma After phase 2 is complete Turn the key / push start — and only now does the car move

Clean English explanation — the full concept

Kör nokta (blind spot) is the area around your car that none of your mirrors can see — specifically, the region just behind and to the side of your car, roughly beside the rear doors. When another vehicle is in that zone, it can be completely invisible to you even though it's just a meter or two away.

Why it happens

Mirrors have a limited field of view. The center of the mirror shows the area directly behind you, and the edges show further out to the sides. But the mirror's view ends at a certain angle — and there's a gap between where the rear mirror's view ends and where your peripheral vision (looking forward) begins. That gap is the kör nokta.

On the top-down diagram on the slide, you can see the two blue shaded cones extending outward from behind the green car — those cones represent the blind-spot zones, and two other vehicles are sitting exactly inside them, invisible to the green-car driver through any mirror.

Why it's dangerous

Lane changes (şerit değiştirme) are the highest-risk situation. You check your side mirror, see "nothing", signal, and start to move over — and bam, there's a car right next to you in the blind spot that you never saw. Classic side-swipe collision scenario.

The 3 ways to compensate (slide content)

  1. Lean slightly toward the steering wheel to widen your mirror's field of view. A small change in head position translates to a big change in mirror angle.
  2. Turn your head briefly to physically look over your shoulder before changing lanes — this is the "omuz üstü bakış" (shoulder check) technique that driving schools teach universally, though the slide emphasizes the mirror-lean method.
  3. Keep your mirrors correctly adjusted. The last line of the slide is the key point: "Doğru pozisyonda ayarlanmamış ayna hiçbir işe yaramaz" — a badly-aimed mirror is worse than useless because it gives you false confidence. A mirror that shows too much of your own car's side is wasting its field of view on your own metal.

"Gidon" is Turkish for motorcycle handlebar, so this is a motorcycle reference: on motorcycles with handlebar-mounted mirrors, the mirrors shake with the bars and require the rider to move their head to see clearly. On a car, the mirrors are fixed to the car body and stay stable — so you can use peripheral vision + slight lean to check them without ever taking your eyes off the road. This is a throwaway point for B-class exam prep but hoca mentioned it, so it's in the notes.

Plain English: This is a more precise explanation than slide 6. The reason the blind spot exists:

Clean English explanation

Dış aynalar (exterior mirrors) — the "sliver of your own car" rule

Adjust each exterior mirror so you see just a small sliver of your own car's rear flank on the inside edge of the mirror. This:

  • Gives you a reference point so you know where the edge of your own car is
  • Maximizes the outward field of view — because most of the mirror is showing the lane next to you, not your own doors
  • Helps minimize the blind spot (slides 6 & 7), though it can't eliminate it

Wrong way: tilting the mirror inward so you see half your own car — that's wasting half the mirror on something you already know the position of.

İç (dikiz) ayna (interior rearview mirror)

Adjust the dikiz aynası (interior mirror) so that with your head in the normal driving position (not leaning forward or sideways), you see the entire rear window framed in the mirror. The slide's instruction "kafanızı ortalayabilirsiniz" literally means "you can center your head" — i.e., set the mirror while sitting normally with your head in its natural driving position. Don't lean while adjusting, or the mirror will only work when you lean.

Vitesin boşta olduğunu kontrol etme (checking for neutral)

Before starting the engine on a manual transmission (düz vites) car, you must verify the gear is in neutral (boş). The slide gives a simple test:

Vites kolunu sağa sola hareket ettir. Sağa ve sola rahat hareket ediyorsa vites boştadır.
Move the gear lever left and right. If it moves freely left and right, the gear is in neutral.

Why this works: when the gear lever is engaged in any gear (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, R), it's locked into the slot for that gear — it can't swing side-to-side, it can only slide forward-back within its column. In neutral, the lever is sitting in the central corridor between all the gear positions, so it can swing freely left and right. If it swings, you're in neutral; if it won't swing, you're still in gear.

Critical safety reason: if you crank the starter while in gear (e.g., 1st gear) with the clutch released, the car will lurch forward as soon as the engine catches — because the engine is already connected to the wheels. If the car is parked close to a wall, another car, or a pedestrian, this can cause damage or injury. Always verify neutral before starting.

On automatic (otomatik vites) cars, the equivalent is to make sure the shifter is in P (Park) or N (Neutral) before starting — most automatics won't even crank unless they're in one of those two positions (a safety interlock).

These are the 3 kırmızı ikaz lambası — all three must be OFF before you start driving. If any of them stays on after startup, the car should not be driven.

Position Turkish English Foot Function
Left Debriyaj Clutch Sol ayak (left foot) Disengages the engine from the transmission so you can shift gears
Middle Fren Brake Sağ ayak (right foot) Slows/stops the car
Right Gaz Gas / Accelerator Sağ ayak (right foot) Opens the throttle, makes the engine produce more power

Foot-assignment rule — absolutely critical for the exam

Sol ayak → sadece debriyaj. Sağ ayak → hem fren hem gaz.
Left foot → only the clutch. Right foot → both brake and gas.

Why right foot handles both brake and gas: because you never need to press the brake and gas at the same time. It's one or the other — speeding up or slowing down. By using the same foot for both, you force yourself to make a clean decision between the two, and it's physically impossible to accidentally press both together.

Why left foot is only for the clutch: you can't use the left foot for braking because the clutch has to be ready for every gear change, and because using the left foot to brake is an unwanted habit that leads to "brake-and-gas at the same time" — which burns out brakes and wastes fuel.

Exam trap question:
- "Düz vitesli araçta fren pedalı hangi ayakla kullanılır?" → Sağ ayak (right foot)
- "Düz vitesli araçta debriyaj pedalı hangi ayakla kullanılır?" → Sol ayak (left foot)
- "Sol ayak hangi pedala basar?" → Debriyaj (clutch)

Automatic transmission exception

On otomatik vitesli (automatic) cars, there is no clutch pedal — only brake and gas. Both are operated by the right foot. The left foot stays on the dead pedal (ayak dayama yeri) the entire time, used only for bracing the body against the seat (slide 3, rule 3).

Reference: e-kitap/03-araç-tekniği/01-aracla-ilgili-temel-bilgiler.md — the portal explicitly says: "Otomatik vitese sahip araçlarda manuel araçlardan farklı olarak vites sürücü tarafından değiştirilmiyor... bu nedenle otomatik vitesli araçlarda debriyaj bulunmuyor. Sol tarafta fren, sağ tarafta ise gaz pedalı bulunuyor."

Pre-start safety: press clutch + brake

The slide ends with an important safety rule:

Aracı çalıştırmadan önce sol ayağınızı debriyaja, sağ ayağınızı frene basın.
Before starting the car, press the clutch with your left foot and the brake with your right foot.

Why this is a safety precaution (tedbir):

  1. Clutch pressed down = engine is physically disconnected from the transmission. Even if the car was accidentally left in gear, it cannot lurch forward when the engine catches.
  2. Brake pressed down = even if the parking brake is weak or released, the car cannot roll in either direction.

So the safe starting posture is: left foot on clutch (floor), right foot on brake, key turns in ignition. On many modern cars this is actually required — the car won't crank at all unless the clutch pedal is pressed (a clutch-start interlock switch).

Clean English explanation — step by step

Step 1 — Release the handbrake (el freni indirme)

The handbrake (el freni / park freni) in most Turkish manual cars is a lever between the two front seats. It has a button (düğme) on top that must be pressed to release the ratcheting mechanism. The correct release procedure:

  1. Press the button on top of the lever with your thumb — this disengages the ratchet
  2. Pull the lever slightly up first (just 1–2 cm) — this releases the pawl
  3. Lower it all the way down (into the flat/rest position)

Why the "pull up first" step: if you just press the button and push the lever down without pulling up first, the mechanism can jam or catch. The brief upward pull unloads the pawl so the ratchet can slide past it cleanly.

Dashboard confirmation: when the handbrake is fully released, the "el freni ışığı" (handbrake warning light, usually a red circle with "!" or "P") goes out on the dashboard. If it stays on, the handbrake isn't fully released — do NOT try to drive off, because you'll burn the rear balatas (see 09-fren-sistemi.md slide 3, fault 1: "El freni çekik unutulursa balatalar ısınır, yanar").

Exam trigger phrase:
- "El freni lambası yanmaya devam ediyorsa ne yapılır?" → El frenini tamamen indir.
- "El freni çekik unutulursa ne olur?" → Balatalar yanar, fren tutmaz olur.

Step 2 — Shift into first gear (birinci vites)

With the clutch still pressed fully down (from slide 12's safety position), move the vites kolu (gear lever) to the 1st gear position. On most H-pattern manual gearboxes, 1st is in the top-left position.

Why first gear and not second: 1st gear has the highest mechanical advantage (lowest gear ratio) — it gives you the maximum torque multiplication, which is what you need to get a stationary car moving against friction, inertia, and any slight slope. Starting in 2nd gear is possible on flat ground but requires more clutch slip and wears out the clutch faster.

Step 3 — The clutch-gas-brake coordination (the hard part)

This is the kalkış (move-off) procedure and it's the single hardest skill for beginner manual drivers:

  1. Sol ayağı yavaşça debriyajdan çek → slowly lift your left foot off the clutch. Slowly is the key word.
  2. Sağ ayağı frenden kaldır → simultaneously lift your right foot off the brake.
  3. Hafifçe gaz pedalına bas → gently press the gas pedal with your right foot.

The timing is what matters:
- The clutch must come up gradually, not fast — it engages the engine to the transmission
- The gas must come on at the same time as the clutch rises — to give the engine enough power not to stall
- If you release the clutch too fast → the engine stalls (araç stop eder)
- If you don't give enough gas → the engine also stalls
- If you give too much gas with the clutch still down → the engine revs without moving the car, wearing out the clutch plate

There's a sweet spot called the "yarım kontak" (half-clutch / friction point) where the clutch is partially engaged and the car starts to creep forward. At the yarım kontak point, you hold the clutch steady and let the car inch forward while the engine pulls against the load. This is the critical skill that takes 10–20 hours of practice to master.

Exam angle: "yarım kontak" süresi hesaplanır mı? — for the exam, you just need to know what yarım kontak is (the partial-engagement point) and that it's used for starting on a slope and slow maneuvering. The actual control of it is tested on the direksiyon uygulama sınavı (practical driving test), not the theory exam.

Part 1 — AKTİF GÜVENLİK SİSTEMLERİ (Active Safety Systems)

The core principle

Aktif sistemler sürüş emniyetini artırarak şoföre yardımcı olan sistemlerdir.
Active systems are systems that increase driving safety by assisting the driver.

They work before an accident happens — their job is to prevent the crash in the first place. An active system is in the "help me NOT crash" category.

The 3 goals of active safety

Every active safety system on a car is trying to do one of these 3 things:

# Turkish English What it prevents
1 Fren güvenliği Brake safety Wheels locking up during hard braking → loss of steering control
2 Patinaj önleme Traction / skid prevention Drive wheels spinning on slippery surfaces → loss of acceleration and direction
3 Savrulma – devrilme kontrolü Swerving / rollover control Car skidding sideways or rolling over during sharp turns

The 4 technology names you MUST memorize

These 4 system acronyms are guaranteed exam material — you should be able to match each name to what it does:

1. ABS — Anti-lock Braking System

Full name: Anti-blokaj Sistem / Kızaklama Önleyici Fren Sistemi (Anti-lock / Anti-skid brake system)

What it does: During hard braking, prevents the wheels from locking up (stopping their rotation entirely). A locked wheel slides along the road instead of rolling, which means:
- You lose steering control (a sliding wheel can't be steered)
- Braking distance actually INCREASES (sliding friction is less than rolling friction)

ABS continuously releases and re-applies brake pressure many times per second at each wheel, keeping them right at the edge of locking but never actually locked. This lets you brake hard AND steer at the same time — critical for emergency avoidance maneuvers.

Symptom of ABS active: brake pedal pulsates / vibrates under your foot, and you may hear a "grrrr" sound. This is normal — it means ABS is working, not faulty. See the hoca's note from earlier sessions.

Relevant exam tie-in: goes under fren güvenliği (goal 1).

2. ASR — Traction Control (Patinaj Önleme)

Full name: Patinaj Önleme / Çekiş Kontrol Sistemi

What it does: Stops the drive wheels from spinning freely on slippery surfaces (ice, wet leaves, gravel) when you accelerate too hard. If ASR detects wheel spin:
- Reduces engine power temporarily
- Brakes the spinning wheel briefly
- Transfers torque to the wheel with more grip

Why it matters: without ASR, pressing the gas too hard on a wet road will spin the drive wheels and the car won't accelerate — it just sits there making noise while the tires smoke. ASR feeds just enough power to actually move the car.

Relevant exam tie-in: goes under patinaj önleme (goal 2). Also sometimes listed as TRC/TC/TCS.

3. VSC / VDC / ESP / ESC — Vehicle Stability Control

Full name: Araç Stabilite Kontrol Sistemi / Elektronik Denge Sistemi

What it does: Prevents the car from skidding sideways or rolling over during sharp turns or sudden maneuvers. If VSC detects the car is starting to slide:
- Individually brakes each wheel as needed to correct the slide
- Reduces engine power if needed
- Pulls the car back onto its intended path

Why it matters: VSC is the most sophisticated active safety system and has saved countless lives. If you're rounding a curve too fast and start to understeer (push wide) or oversteer (spin), VSC will quietly intervene and keep the car on the road.

Many names for the same thing (two-name trap):
- VSC — Vehicle Stability Control (Toyota)
- VDC — Vehicle Dynamics Control (Nissan, Subaru)
- ESP — Elektronik Stabilite Programı (European, VW/Mercedes)
- ESC — Electronic Stability Control (general)

All of these are the same system with different manufacturer names.

Relevant exam tie-in: goes under savrulma-devrilme kontrolü (goal 3).

4. EMS — Electronically Controlled Suspension

Full name: Elektronik Kontrollü Süspansiyon Sistemi

What it does: Adjusts the amortisör (shock absorber) firmness in real time based on driving conditions:
- Softer for comfort on smooth roads
- Firmer for control during hard cornering or braking
- Each wheel can be adjusted independently

Why it's aktif güvenlik: by keeping all four wheels in optimal contact with the road regardless of driving conditions, EMS indirectly helps the brakes, steering, and traction systems work better. It's a supporting system for the other three.

Relevant exam tie-in: EMS enables goals 1, 2, and 3 by giving the other systems a stable foundation.

Part 2 — PASİF GÜVENLİK SİSTEMLERİ (Passive Safety Systems)

The core principle

Pasif güvenlik sistemleri, aracın ve yolcuların kaza anında ve sonrasında güvenliğini sağlayan sistemlerdir.
Passive safety systems protect the vehicle and its occupants during and after a crash.

They work during and after an accident — their job is to reduce injuries when the crash has already started or finished. A passive system is in the "help me survive when the crash is happening" category.

The 7 passive safety components

This is a memorize-all-7 exam question. You should be able to list them cleanly:

1. Airbag (Hava Yastığı)

A nylon bag folded inside the steering wheel, dashboard, seats, or headliner that inflates explosively (in ~30 milliseconds) when an impact sensor detects a crash. The airbag:
- Creates a cushion between the occupant and the hard interior surfaces
- Spreads the impact force over a larger area of the body
- Deflates quickly after the impact so you're not trapped

Types: sürücü airbag (driver), yolcu airbag (passenger), yan airbag (side), perde airbag (curtain), diz airbag (knee). Modern cars have 6–10 airbags.

Critical rule: airbags only work if the seatbelt is on. Airbag deployment force alone can injure an unbelted occupant. Emniyet kemeri + airbag = full protection. Just airbag = dangerous.

2. Ön germeli emniyet kemerleri (Pre-tensioning seatbelts)

A modern seatbelt has a pyrotechnic pre-tensioner — a small explosive charge that pulls the seatbelt tight in the first milliseconds of a crash, before the occupant starts to move forward. This:
- Removes any slack in the belt
- Locks the occupant firmly into the seat
- Prepares the body for the airbag deployment

Then, the belt has a load limiter that slightly releases tension if the force on the belt exceeds a safe threshold — preventing broken ribs while still keeping the occupant in place.

Plain English: "ön germeli" = "front-tensioning" = the belt tightens forcefully at the start of a crash, then controls the release.

3. Arttırılmış kabin mukavemeti (Reinforced passenger cell)

The passenger safety cell — the rigid box of steel around where the occupants sit. Made of ultra high strength steel (UHSS) and boron steel (see 11-süspansiyon-sistemi.md slide 7 on Günümüz Otomobil Yapısı). Its job is to resist deformation during a crash, so the occupants' survival space is preserved even as the front and rear of the car crumple.

This ties directly to the "şase" concept — the rigid cabin + crumple zones idea from slide 4 of the süspansiyon file.

4. Kapı içi çelik barlar (Door impact beams)

Steel reinforcement bars welded into the inside of every car door. Their job: resist side-impact intrusion. If another car T-bones yours, the door impact beams prevent the door from being pushed into the occupant's body. Often made of bor çelik (boron steel) for maximum strength.

5. Teleskopik katlanır direksiyon kolonu (Telescopic collapsing steering column)

The direksiyon mili (steering shaft) is designed to collapse / telescope inward during a frontal crash. This prevents the steering wheel from being pushed into the driver's chest by the engine bay deforming. Instead, the wheel stays where it is while the shaft compresses behind it — a "broken pencil" design where the shaft folds or slides into itself.

Before this invention, drivers were commonly killed or severely injured by the steering column being driven into their chest during head-on collisions. The collapsing column was one of the most important safety innovations of the 20th century.

6. Kaza esnasında arkaya katlanır pedal sistemi (Pedals fold back during crash)

In a frontal crash, the engine bay crumples and pushes inward — without this system, the pedals (debriyaj, fren, gaz) would be pushed backward into the driver's legs and feet, causing severe lower-leg and foot injuries.

Modern cars have a collapsible pedal mechanism where the pedals are designed to break away and fold out of the way toward the floor (or toward the firewall) during a crash impact, preventing leg injuries.

7. Motor kulak dizaynı (Breakaway engine mounts)

Kaza anında koparak motorun devrilmesini sağlayan motor kulak dizaynı.

Plain English: the motor kulakları (engine mounts) are the rubber-and-metal components that hold the engine to the chassis. In a modern safety design, the mounts are engineered to break away / fail in a controlled way during a frontal crash, allowing the entire engine block to drop downward and backward under the car — away from the passenger compartment.

Why this is critical: without breakaway mounts, the engine would be pushed backward into the firewall, and potentially into the driver's legs and lap. With the breakaway design, the engine falls out of the crash path and ends up under the car rather than inside it.

"Motor kulak" literally means "engine ears" — the Turkish automotive slang for engine mounts (they look like small pointed "ears" sticking out from the engine block).

Quick-reference comparison table

Aktif güvenlik (active) Pasif güvenlik (passive)
Prevents accidents Reduces injuries during accidents
Works before the crash Works during and after the crash
ABS, ASR, VSC, EMS Airbag, emniyet kemeri, kabin, kapı barları, direksiyon kolonu, pedal, motor kulak
"Kazanın olmasını engeller" "Kaza anında yaralanmayı azaltır"

Exam rule of thumb

If an exam question describes a system and asks "aktif mi pasif mi?", apply this mental test:

Would this system help me if the crash NEVER happened?
- Yes → AKTİF (it's preventing the crash)
- No, it only matters when the crash is happening → PASİF (it's managing injury)

Examples:
- ABS → Would it help if I never crashed? Yes, it shortens stopping distance on dry roads too. → AKTİF
- Airbag → Would it help if I never crashed? No, it just sits there doing nothing. → PASİF
- ESP → Would it help if I never crashed? Yes, it keeps me on the road during sharp turns. → AKTİF
- Emniyet kemeri → Would it help if I never crashed? No, it just holds me in place during normal driving. → PASİF

Critical exam facts — memorize ALL of these

Aktif güvenlik = kazayı önleyen sistemler. Pasif güvenlik = kaza anında yaralanmayı azaltan sistemler.

Aktif sistemler: ABS, ASR, VSC (ESP), TRC, EMS.

Pasif sistemler: Airbag, ön germeli emniyet kemeri, kabin mukavemeti, kapı çelik barları, teleskopik direksiyon kolonu, geri katlanır pedallar, motor kulak dizaynı.

Aktif sistemin 3 amacı: fren güvenliği, patinaj önleme, savrulma-devrilme kontrolü.

Rodaj (Break-in Period): The period of time from when the vehicle leaves the factory until it has covered a certain number of kilometers.

  • During the rodaj period, no sudden stops or starts. Don't drive too fast.
  • Rodaj is also the name for the process of the engine parts adapting to each other after the car leaves the factory.

Clean English explanation — what rodaj actually is

When a car rolls off the factory assembly line, its engine parts (pistons, silindirler, biyel kolu, krank mili, vites dişlileri, etc.) are brand new and not yet "worn in" to each other. The machined surfaces still have tiny imperfections that need to smooth out and settle against each other. This is the rodaj (break-in) period.

Purpose of rodaj:
- Let the motor parçaları (engine parts) wear in to each other in a controlled way
- Allow the piston rings to seat properly against the cylinder walls
- Let the bearings (yataklar) establish their running surfaces
- Break in the vites kutusu gears, debriyaj, and fren balataları too

If you abuse a brand-new car during its rodaj period, you can cause permanent damage that reduces the engine's lifespan, increases oil consumption, and reduces power. Done correctly, rodaj extends the engine's life significantly.

The 2 rodaj rules from the slide

Rule 1 — No sudden stops / starts (Ani duruş kalkış yapılmaz)

Plain English: Don't slam the accelerator, don't slam the brakes, don't do hard launches, don't race off from traffic lights. Everything should be smooth and gentle during the rodaj period. Each hard start/stop puts massive load on new engine parts before they've bedded in.

Rule 2 — No high speeds (Fazla sürat yapılmaz)

Plain English: Don't redline the engine. Don't cruise at top speed. Don't do long high-speed highway runs in the first 1000–3000 km. The engine should run at modest RPM (below ~3000 rpm per the portal) and modest speeds.

More rodaj detail (from the portal)

From e-kitap/03-araç-tekniği/02-guvenli-arac-kullanma-arac-bakim-ve-kontrolleri.md, the full rodaj rules are:

  1. First 1000–3000 km: don't exceed 3000 RPM — gentle throttle only
  2. Don't floor the accelerator — pedal to the floor is extremely damaging in rodaj
  3. Let the engine warm up for 30 sn – 1 dk before driving off
  4. First 2000 km: use varying speeds instead of constant cruise — this helps the parts bed in without any single RPM hot spot
  5. Avoid long high-speed stretches — the friction between piston rings and cylinder walls is highest at high speed, and can score the cylinders if done before break-in

The two definitions of rodaj (memorize both)

The slide gives two complementary definitions:

  1. Time/kilometer based: "Fabrika çıkışından sonra belirli kilometre kadar geçen süreye denir" → A period measured from factory departure until a specific km count is reached
  2. Mechanical process based: "Motor parçalarının birbirlerine alışma sürecine denir" → The process of engine parts adapting to each other

Both definitions describe the same thing from different angles — one talks about the time/distance, one talks about what's physically happening inside the engine during that time.

Part 2 — Haftalık Bakım (Weekly Maintenance) — 2 main items

Item 1 — Vantilatör kayışı gerginliği (Fan belt tension)

Vantilatör kayışı = the V-belt / drive belt that runs off the krank mili pulley and drives the radyatör fanı, the alternatör (şarj dinamosu), and sometimes other accessories. Over time the belt stretches and becomes loose — a loose belt slips on the pulleys, causing:

  • Alternator not spinning fast enough → şarj ikazı (charge warning) starts coming on
  • Fan not spinning fast enough → engine overheats in stop-and-go traffic
  • A squealing noise from the engine bay (especially on cold starts)

Item 2 — Akü bakımı (Battery maintenance) — the big one

This is the detailed part of the slide. See breakdown below.

Part 3 — Akü Bakımı (Battery Maintenance) Deep Dive

This is a high-density slide with 7+ specific rules about battery care. Each rule corresponds to a possible exam question.

Rule 1 — Clean oxide with soda + hot water

Akü dış yüzeylerinin ve kutup başlarındaki oksitlerin sodalı su ve sıcak su ile temizlenmesi.

Plain English: Over time, green-white oxidation (battery acid fumes reacting with metal) builds up on the kutup başları (battery terminal posts) and around the top of the battery. This oxidation:

  • Adds electrical resistance at the terminal contacts
  • Reduces the current flowing to the marş motoru (starter)
  • Can cause starting problems and electrical faults

To clean it: use a mixture of sodalı su (soda water = water + baking soda) and sıcak su (hot water). The soda neutralizes the acidic residue, and the hot water + mechanical scrubbing removes the crusty deposits. A stiff-bristled wire brush helps. Afterward, the terminals should be shiny metal.

Why soda water? Because battery acid is sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). Baking soda (NaHCO₃) is a mild base that neutralizes the acid safely. Plain water would spread the acid around without neutralizing it.

Rule 2 — Add distilled water to 1 cm above the plates

Plakaların 1 cm üzerine kadar saf su ilave edilmesi.

Plain English: Traditional (non-sealed) batteries use a sulfuric acid + water electrolyte. Over time, the water evaporates (especially in hot weather and during use), leaving the electrolyte too concentrated. This exposes the plakaları (internal lead plates) to air, which damages them permanently.

The fix: add saf su (distilled water / pure water) — NEVER tap water (tap water has minerals that contaminate the electrolyte). Add just enough water to cover the plates by 1 cm. The "1 cm above the plates" is a specific exam number to memorize.

Why distilled water: tap water contains calcium, iron, chlorine, and other minerals that react with the battery chemistry, reducing capacity and lifespan.

Rule 3 — Keep cell cap holes open

Eleman kapak deliklerinin açık tutulması.

Plain English: Each cell of the battery has a cap (eleman kapağı) with a small vent hole (kapak deliği). During charging, the battery produces hidrojen gazı (hydrogen gas) — an extremely flammable gas. The vent holes let that gas escape harmlessly to the atmosphere.

If the vent holes are clogged (by dirt, corrosion, debris):
- Hydrogen gas builds up inside the battery
- Pressure rises
- The battery can explode from any spark or static discharge

Always keep the vent holes open and clean — check them during weekly bakım.

Rule 4 — Keep the battery fully charged in winter to prevent freezing

Kışın akü donmasın diye akü tam şarj ettirilir.

Plain English: Battery electrolyte is mostly sulfuric acid + water. The freezing point of this mixture depends on its concentration:

  • Fully charged battery → electrolyte is strong acid (~35% sulfuric) → freezes at about −60°C
  • Discharged battery → electrolyte is mostly water → freezes at about −5°C

In winter, a discharged battery can actually freeze in very cold weather. The frozen electrolyte expands and cracks the battery case, destroying it. To prevent this:

  • Keep the battery fully charged throughout winter
  • A full charge = strong electrolyte = very low freezing point = safe

This is why in cold climates, you should run your car regularly in winter to keep the battery topped up, or use a trickle charger.

Rule 5 — Don't jump-start a car with digital dashboard gauges

Dijital göstergeli araçta asla akü takviyesi yapılmaz.

Plain English: This is CRITICAL and often exam-tested. Modern cars with digital / electronic dashboard gauges have sensitive electronic control units (ECU) that can be damaged by voltage spikes during a traditional jump-start.

When you jump-start a car from another car's battery:
- The sudden connection can cause a voltage spike (brief surge above 14V)
- On older cars with analog gauges, this didn't matter
- On modern cars with digital/computerized systems, this spike can fry the ECU, dashboard, or other electronics

The rule: ASLA (NEVER) jump-start a car with a digital gauge panel. Instead:

  • Use a proper battery charger (slow, regulated charging)
  • Tow the car to a service center
  • Have a professional charge the battery properly

Attempting to jump-start a modern computerized car can cause thousands of lira in electronic damage. It's a very expensive mistake.

Exam trigger phrase:
- "Dijital göstergeli araçta akü takviyesi yapılır mı?" → Hayır, asla yapılmaz. Elektronik sistemlere zarar verir.

Rule 6 — Self-discharge = dirt on top of battery

Akü kendiliğinden boşanıyorsa akünün üst kısmında pislik birikmiştir.

Plain English: If your battery discharges by itself when the car is sitting parked (overnight / over a weekend), the cause is usually dirt, moisture, and oxide buildup on the top surface of the battery. This creates a faint electrical path between the two terminals across the top of the battery case, causing a slow self-discharge over time.

The fix: clean the top of the battery thoroughly with soda water (rule 1) and keep it dry.

Exam trigger phrase:
- "Akü kendiliğinden boşalıyorsa sebebi nedir?" → Akünün üst kısmında pislik birikmiştir.

Rule 7 — Fire from short circuit → remove terminal posts

Kısa devreden dolayı yangın olursa akü kutup başları çıkarılır.

Plain English: If a short circuit anywhere in the car's electrical system starts a fire, the first thing to do is disconnect the battery — specifically, remove both kutup başları (terminal posts) from the battery. This:

  • Cuts the power source feeding the short-circuit fire
  • Prevents the fire from spreading via more electrical arcing
  • Buys you time to extinguish it

Order of disconnection: remove the eksi (−, negative) kutup first, then the artı (+, positive). This prevents accidental short-circuits while you're working.

Exam trigger phrase:
- "Kısa devre nedeniyle araçta yangın çıkarsa ne yapılır?" → Akünün kutup başları çıkarılır (akü bağlantısı kesilir).

Rule 8 — Never place metal across both terminals → explosion risk

Akü 2 kutup başı arasında her iki kutup başına değen bir madeni parça konsa akü kısa devre olup patlar.

Plain English: This is a safety warning. If you place any metal object (wrench, screwdriver, key, tool) in a position where it touches both kutup başları (terminal posts) at the same time, you create a direct short circuit through that metal object.

What happens in a short circuit:
- The battery tries to dump its entire stored energy through the metal object instantly
- The metal gets red-hot in a fraction of a second
- The electrolyte inside the battery boils and pressurizes
- The battery explodes, spraying sulfuric acid and battery case fragments

This can cause:
- Severe acid burns to your face, eyes, and body
- Blindness
- Flying shrapnel injuries
- Fire

Safety rule: when working around the battery, keep tools away from the terminals. If you must use a tool on one terminal, cover or remove the other terminal first. This is why mechanics always disconnect the eksi (negative) terminal first — to eliminate the short-circuit risk while working on the artı (positive) side.

Exam trigger phrase:
- "Akünün iki kutup başına madeni parça değdirilirse ne olur?" → Akü kısa devre olup patlar. Çok tehlikelidir.

  • If heavy snow or freezing is expected, buy a snow chain (kar zinciri / patinaj zinciri) early — otherwise you'll end up having to buy one at black-market prices (karaborsa).
  • Always bring your mobile phone's car charger with you. Or bring a backup battery / power bank.

The 6 winter preparation items — detail

Item 1 — Radyatör / antifriz check

Plain English: Before winter, verify your radiator has antifreeze (antifriz) at the correct concentration. If the concentration is too weak (diluted from adding water over the year), take it to a service to be tested and topped up. Antifreeze lowers the freezing point of the coolant — without it, the coolant can freeze, expand, and crack the engine block (which is a catastrophic failure requiring a full engine rebuild).

The term "tüver yaptırın" in the slide is Turkish auto-shop slang for "takviye yaptırın" — "have it topped up / reinforced".

See [slide 19 below] for the full antifriz definition.

Item 2 — Weather forecasts

Plain English: Check meteorological weather reports (hava tahmin raporları) more actively in winter. Snow, ice, and fog can dramatically change driving conditions within hours. Don't get caught off guard — check the forecast before every morning commute.

Sources in Turkey: MGM (Meteoroloji Genel Müdürlüğü), mgm.gov.tr, weather apps.

Item 3 — Silecek suyu (wiper fluid) with anti-freeze additive

Plain English: Regular silecek suyu (windshield washer fluid) is mostly water. In freezing weather, it will freeze in the reservoir and in the supply tubes, and when you try to spray it, nothing comes out. Worse, the freezing expansion can crack the plastic reservoir.

The fix: add a winter washer fluid concentrate (an alcohol-based chemical) to the silecek suyu. This lowers the freezing point of the wiper fluid, so it stays liquid down to −15°C or −20°C depending on the concentration.

Common choices: commercial "kış silecek suyu" bottles sold at fuel stations, or methanol/isopropyl alcohol mixes.

Item 4 — Lift the wipers off the glass

Plain English: If the car is going to sit outside overnight in freezing temperatures, pull the wiper arms up away from the windshield so the rubber blades don't freeze to the glass. If you don't:

  • The blades freeze solid to the glass
  • In the morning you turn on the wipers
  • The motor strains against the frozen blade
  • Either the wiper motor burns out OR the rubber of the blade rips off OR both
  • Either way you now have a broken wiper

Lifting the wipers is a 2-second precaution that prevents expensive damage.

Bonus tip: if possible, park in a kapalı bir yer (enclosed / covered parking) — a garage, under a canopy, in a parking structure. This eliminates most cold-weather problems entirely.

Item 5 — Buy the snow chain EARLY

Plain English: Kar zinciri (patinaj zinciri) = snow chain. In Turkey, as soon as it snows heavily, demand spikes and snow chains either sell out or go to karaborsa (black market) at inflated prices. Smart drivers buy their snow chain in autumn (Ekim-Kasım) before the first snowfall and keep it in the trunk.

Key vocabulary:
- Kar zinciri / patinaj zinciri = snow chain
- Karaborsa = black market (things sold under the counter at inflated prices)

See [slide 21 below] for installation details.

Item 6 — Phone car charger + backup battery

Plain English: If you get stuck in snow (stranded) in a remote area or on a blocked highway, your phone is your lifeline for calling for help. A dead phone in a winter breakdown is dangerous.

Precautions:
- Always have the araç şarj cihazı (car charger) in the glove box
- Or carry a yedek batarya / power bank charged and ready
- Don't let your phone drop below 50% battery in winter
- Know the emergency number: 112 (universal emergency)

Clean English explanation — what antifreeze actually does

Antifriz is NOT pure water. It's a carefully balanced mixture of:

  • Water (su) — the primary coolant, carries heat away from the engine
  • Ethylene glycol (etilen glikol) — the chemical additive that modifies the water's physical properties
  • Corrosion inhibitors — protect metal parts from rust
  • Colorant — usually green, pink, red, or blue (different manufacturers use different colors)

The magic is in mixing water and ethylene glycol together — the combination has very different physical properties from either alone.

The 3 jobs of antifreeze

Job 1 — Lowers the freezing point (donma derecesini düşürür)

Pure water freezes at 0 °C. A 50/50 water-ethylene-glycol mix freezes at about −37 °C. Higher glycol concentrations can push the freezing point down to about −53 °C (the slide's stated minimum).

Why this matters: if the water in your radiator freezes, it expands by 9%, which can:
- Crack the motor bloğu (engine block) — a multi-thousand-lira catastrophic failure
- Burst the radiator and its tanks
- Split the hoses
- Rupture the water pump (su pompası)

A car with proper antifriz won't freeze even in the coldest Turkish winters — Erzurum, Ağrı, Kars.

Job 2 — Raises the boiling point (kaynama derecesini yükseltir)

Pure water boils at 100 °C. A 50/50 antifreeze mix boils at about 107 °C (pressurized in the radiator, more like 125 °C). Higher concentrations push it even higher.

Why this matters: modern engines run hot — coolant temperatures of 90–105 °C are normal. If your cooling system used pure water, it would be right at the boiling point during normal operation, and any small increase would cause boiling and overheating. Antifreeze provides a safety margin of 10–20 °C above normal operating temperature.

Job 3 — Protects against corrosion, rust, and limescale

Covered in 01-yakıt-sistemi.md / portal e-kitap: "Antifriz donmayı önlemenin yanı sıra soğutma sıvısının geçtiği yerlerde korozyonu, paslanmayı ve kireç oluşumunu da engelleyerek parçaların ömrünü artırmaktadır."

Pure water + metal = rust over time. Pure water + minerals = limescale (kireç) that clogs the radiator and coolant passages. Antifreeze contains chemical inhibitors that:
- Passivate metal surfaces (stops oxidation)
- Chelate mineral ions (prevents them from forming deposits)
- Buffer pH (keeps the mixture neutral to slightly alkaline)

This is the reason antifreeze is used ALL YEAR ROUND, not just in winter. Even in summer, you need antifreeze for the corrosion and boiling protection.

The "zehirli / toxic" warning

Etilen glikol çok zehirli bir maddedir.
Ethylene glycol is highly toxic.

Ethylene glycol is extremely poisonous — lethal dose for an adult is about 100 ml (less for children or pets). Tragically, it tastes sweet, which is why cats and dogs sometimes drink it from puddles on the ground and die. Humans have also accidentally poisoned themselves with antifreeze, or have been poisoned by others using it as a murder weapon (it's a recurring topic in criminal cases).

Safety rules:
- Never leave antifreeze in open containers where children or pets can reach it
- Clean up spills immediately with absorbent material + plenty of water
- Don't siphon antifreeze from a radiator using your mouth (classic mistake)
- Dispose of used antifreeze at a hazardous waste facility, not down the drain

The chemical formula

HO(CH₂CH₂O)ₙH — ethylene glycol (or polyethylene glycol) formula

The "n" subscript indicates it's actually a polymer form of ethylene glycol, which is why the slide writes it as a repeating unit. Pure ethylene glycol is HOCH₂CH₂OH (n=1). Antifreeze uses the monomer + water mixture.

You don't need to memorize the chemistry — just know that ethylene glycol is the active ingredient.

The −53 °C number

En düşük donma derecesi yaklaşık −53 derecedir.
The lowest freezing point is approximately −53 °C.

This is a memorize-the-number exam fact. At the maximum useful concentration of ethylene glycol (about 68%), a water + glycol mixture freezes at −53 °C. Lower concentrations give higher freezing points:

Water : Glycol Freezing point
100% water 0 °C
70% : 30% −16 °C
50% : 50% −37 °C
40% : 60% −53 °C (approximately the max)
30% : 70% starts to rise again (too little water to dissolve the glycol)

Note: you don't want a glycol concentration above ~70% — the mixture becomes more viscous and actually provides worse cooling because glycol has lower heat capacity than water.

The 8 winter driving rules — detail

Rule 1 — Don't drive if you don't have to

Plain English: Rule #1 of winter driving is: if you can avoid it, don't drive. Use public transport, stay home, work remotely. The safest snow-driving strategy is not driving. This is also reinforced by the next point — even if you're careful, other drivers may not be, so your safety isn't fully in your hands.

Rule 2 — Other drivers are the biggest risk

Plain English: You can be the most cautious driver in the world, but if the driver next to you slides into your lane, you're still in an accident. Winter roads have many inexperienced drivers panicking, making mistakes, and losing control. Give other cars extra space — double or triple the normal following distance.

Rule 3 — Park somewhere sheltered + lift wipers overnight

Already covered in slide 18 (item 4) — lift wipers, park in covered area if possible.

Rule 4 — Clear ALL snow and ice before driving

Plain English: Before driving off in winter:

  1. Clear ALL snow from the windshield, all side and rear windows, and both dış aynalar (side mirrors)
  2. Clear ice — use an ice scraper (buz kazıyıcı), not hot water (hot water on ice-cold glass can crack the glass)
  3. Clear snow from the roof — otherwise chunks will slide forward onto your windshield when you brake
  4. Clear snow from the headlights and taillights — you need to be visible
  5. Use buz çözücü solüsyon (de-icer spray) for stubborn ice
  6. Add buz çözücü additive to the washer fluid

Exam angle: "Karlı havada yola çıkmadan önce ne yapılır?" → Tüm camlar, aynalar, farlar kardan ve buzdan temizlenir.

Rule 5 — Wait for engine warm-up

Plain English: In cold weather, the engine needs longer than 30 seconds to warm up properly — more like 1–3 minutes. Let the engine reach a stable idle before driving off. A cold engine has thick oil that can't lubricate properly, and revving it hard before warm-up causes premature wear.

This ties back to slide 11 of this file about the normal 30-second-1-minute warm-up, but in winter it should be longer.

Rule 6 — Winter tires with adequate tread depth

Plain English: Kış lastikleri (winter tires) are a legal requirement in some Turkish provinces during winter months for commercial vehicles, and strongly recommended for everyone. Winter tires:

  • Have deeper tread (minimum 4 mm by law, vs 3 mm for summer — see 08b-lastikler.md)
  • Use a softer rubber compound that stays flexible in cold temperatures (summer tires harden below 7 °C and lose grip)
  • Have sipes (small slits in the tread blocks) that bite into snow and ice
  • Are marked with a snowflake + mountain symbol on the sidewall

Legal minimum tread depths:

Season Minimum tread
Yaz lastiği (summer) 3 mm
Kış lastiği (winter) 4 mm
Legal absolute minimum (any tire) 1.6 mm

Reference: 08b-lastikler.md for the full tire discussion.

Rule 7 — Smooth driving, no sudden inputs

Plain English: On snow/ice, the friction between tire and road is tiny. Any sudden input (hard brake, hard steering, hard gas) breaks that friction and the car starts sliding. Winter driving technique:

  • Accelerate gently — no "hard launches". On ice, pressing the gas too hard just spins the drive wheels without moving the car forward.
  • Brake gently and early — leave 5× the normal following distance. Use engine braking (downshifting) instead of or together with the foot brake.
  • Steer gently — smooth, slow steering inputs. No jerking the wheel.
  • If you must brake hard — ABS will help (pulsing pedal), but braking distance is still 3–5× longer on ice than on dry asphalt.

Aracın yere tutunmasını sağlayın = "keep the car stuck to the road" → minimize anything that breaks contact between tires and asphalt.

Rule 8 — Defrost the windows with the heater

Plain English: Winter cabin = humid breathing + cold windows = buğu (fog/condensation) on the inside of all the glass. To keep visibility clear:

  • Kalorifer (heater) set to full hot
  • Fan set to high
  • Air direction to ön cam (windshield) + yan camlar (side windows)
  • If available, use the klima (A/C) alongside the heater — the A/C removes humidity from the cabin air, clearing fog much faster
  • On many modern cars there's a dedicated defroster / buğu çözücü button that automatically sets this up

Exam trigger phrase:
- "Araç camlarında buğu oluştuğunda ne yapılır?" → Kalorifer sıcak konumda çalıştırılır ve hava ön cama yönlendirilir. Klima da açılabilir.

Clean English explanation — high level

Modern snow chains are color-coded with red / yellow / green plastic coatings on different parts to make installation easier — you just follow the colors in order.

  2. Lock the red section behind the rim
  3. Attach the yellow cable to red points
  4. Tighten by pulling

  6. Route the final tensioner through the guide sleeve
  7. Drive 100 m, then re-tighten (because the chain settles and loosens during the first few meters)

The "çekiş olduğu lastik" rule — which wheels to install on

The first word of the slide is critical: "çekiş olduğu lastiğin arkasından" — "behind the drive wheel".

Kar zinciri sadece çekici (drive) lastiklere takılır.

  • Önden çekişli (FWD) araç → kar zinciri ön iki tekerleğe takılır (most modern cars)
  • Arkadan çekişli (RWD) araç → kar zinciri arka iki tekerleğe takılır (most trucks, some older cars)
  • 4 çekişli (4WD/AWD) araç → ideally all 4, in practice just the front 2 if you only have one set

You install the chain on the wheels that GET POWER from the engine, not the other ones — because the other wheels are just "along for the ride" and chains there don't help you accelerate, climb hills, or get unstuck.

The "100 m sonra tekrar ger" rule

Plain English: After installing the chain and driving the first 100 meters, stop and re-tighten the chain. This is because:

  • When first installed, the chain is positioned but not fully seated
  • During the first few meters of driving, the chain settles into the tire tread and shifts around
  • This causes the chain to become slightly loose
  • A loose chain can fly off the wheel at high speed, damaging the car's wheel well, brake lines, or suspension

Always re-check + re-tighten at ~100 m. After that, the chain should stay tight for the rest of the trip.

Plain English — what yokuş kalkış desteği is:

Yokuş kalkış desteği (Hill Start Assist / HSA / HHC) is a feature where:

  • You stop the car on an uphill slope (e.g., at a red light on a hill)
  • You release the brake pedal
  • For about 2 seconds, the car automatically holds the brakes on — preventing the car from rolling backward
  • In those 2 seconds, you press the gas pedal (or, on a manual, engage the clutch)
  • The car takes off forward cleanly
  • The held brake releases once the engine torque is enough to propel the car uphill

Result: the car doesn't roll back even by a centimeter during the 2-second window.

Why this is a big deal for ehliyet candidates

The direksiyon sınavı (practical driving exam) includes a yokuş kalkış test — the committee parks you on a hill and asks you to move off. If the car rolls backward even 30 cm, you fail the exam.

For manuel vites (stick-shift) candidates

On a manual car without yokuş kalkış desteği, you must perform the yarım debriyaj (half-clutch) hill start technique:

  1. Car stopped on the hill, clutch fully pressed, in 1st gear, foot on brake
  2. Start releasing clutch until you feel the yarım debriyaj point
  3. At that exact moment, release the brake (car doesn't roll because half-clutch holds it)
  4. Press gas + release clutch fully
  5. Car moves forward without rolling back

This is HARD. Beginners roll back several meters until they learn it. The committee expects zero rollback — so manual drivers must perfect this technique through practice.

For otomatik vites candidates

On an otomatik with HSA, you just:

  1. Press the brake, stop on the hill
  2. Release the brake (HSA holds the car)
  3. Press gas — car moves off smoothly
  4. Zero rollback, zero skill required

This is why otomatik ehliyet is significantly easier for the direksiyon sınavı — the hardest skill on the exam (hill start) is automatic.

Why it qualifies as "aktif güvenlik"

Using the test from slide 15:

"Would this system help me if the accident NEVER happened?"
Yes — it helps me drive off smoothly on hills, every day, regardless of any accident. It's assisting my driving, not protecting me in a crash. → AKTİF güvenlik.

Already partially covered in slide 17 / rule 8 of akü bakımı, but hoca re-emphasized in very direct terms:

"Yalnız aküyle eğer uğraşacaksanız elinizde bir metal yüzüktür, saattir vesairedir bunları elinizden çıkartmanız lazım. Değmemesi lazım. Kısa devre yapar yoksa."

Plain English: Before working on the battery (adding water, cleaning, jump-starting, any hands-on):

  • Remove metal rings (yüzük) from your fingers
  • Remove metal watches (saat) from your wrist
  • Remove metal bracelets, chains, necklaces — anything conductive on your hands/arms

Why: if you accidentally touch both battery terminals (+ and −) with a metal object while it's still touching your skin, the object becomes a short circuit — and it instantly heats to thousands of degrees. A metal ring on your finger that shorts across terminals will:

  • Burn into your skin (instant severe burn)
  • Weld itself to the battery posts — you can't pull your hand away
  • The current flows through YOU instead of the battery
  • The battery explodes from the internal short

This is how people lose fingers or get third-degree burns from battery maintenance. Just take the jewelry off. Takes 5 seconds and saves your hand.

Addition 3 — ⭐ Winter prep: go down to the car 10 min EARLIER than usual

Already in slide 18 in general, but hoca gave a specific timing rule:

"Kışa veya ki yola çıkacaksam normal çıktığım saatten bir on dakika daha önce aşağı ineyim arabamın başına. Kar varsa sağında solunda küreyim. Arabayı birazcık ısıtayım değil mi? Bir beş, on dakika. Bunu zaten hani görürsünüz. Bunları yapmanız lazım."

Plain English: On winter mornings when you have somewhere to be, leave home 10 minutes earlier than your usual departure time. Use those 10 minutes to:

  1. Shovel / brush snow from around the car (and from ON the car — roof, windows, hood, trunk)
  2. Clear ice from the windshield with a scraper
  3. Start the engine and let it run to warm up for 5–10 minutes
  4. Let the kalorifer defrost the windows from the inside
  5. Check the tire pressures (cold-weather pressure drops)

Then you drive off with:
- A clear windshield
- A warm cabin
- A properly lubricated engine
- No ice on the glass

Without these 10 minutes, you either drive with ice-blind glass (dangerous and illegal), or you arrive at work 15 minutes late because you did all this at the last second while stressed.

Winter rule: 10 dakika erken in, aracı hazırla, rahat git.

Addition 5 — "Emanuel kullanan arkadaşım ne yapacak?" — the manual driver's disadvantage

Hoca contrasted manual drivers with automatic drivers explicitly:

"Emanuel kullanan arkadaşım ne yapacak? Debriyajı ayarlamak zorunda ayağıyla birlikte. Yani o kavramayı kendisi ayarlamak zorunda. Ayar biraz önce bahsettim ya, çok aşağıda olursa araç geriye doğru kayacak ama bunu kaydırmamanız gerekiyor."

Plain English: On a manuel (stick-shift) car, the driver must manually control the yarım debriyaj position with their left foot during:

  • Hill starts
  • Slow maneuvers in parking
  • Creeping through heavy traffic
  • Moving off from a complete stop

If the clutch release timing is wrong (released too fast, or not enough gas, or not at the friction point), the car either:
- Stalls (engine dies)
- Rolls backward (on a hill)
- Jerks forward (too much gas)

On an automatic, none of this applies — the car handles clutch coordination automatically.

This is why the yarım debriyaj skill takes dozens of practice lessons to master, and why hoca kept emphasizing it throughout class 2.

5. Why this matters for the exam

Typical exam question patterns:

  1. "Araca binmeden önce hangi kontroller yapılır?" → Lastikler, fren/stop lambaları, sinyaller, benzin deposu kapağı, yağ akıntısı, aracın altı (kedi-köpek).
  2. "Aracın altı neden kontrol edilir?" → Kedi, köpek veya herhangi bir engelin olmadığını görmek için.
  3. "Sürücü koltuğunun sırt açısı kaç derece olmalıdır?" → Yaklaşık 90°.
  4. "Sürüş esnasında sol ayak nerede olmalıdır?" → Ayak dayama yerinde (dead pedal), vücudu koltuğa yapıştıracak şekilde.
  5. "Koltuk ileri-geri ayarı nasıl yapılmalıdır?" → Kol uzatıldığında bilek direksiyonun üst noktasına gelmelidir.
  6. "Doğru sürüş pozisyonunun faydası nedir?" → Reaksiyon süresini kısaltır, sert manevralarda öne savrulmayı önler, vücudun rahat hareket etmesini sağlar.
  7. "Sürüş sırasında omuzlar koltuk sırtından ayrılırsa ne olur?" → Araç kontrolü zorlaşır, direksiyon üzerinde yeterli güç uygulanamaz.

6. Memorize These

Araca binmeden önce: lastik, fren lambası, stop lambası, sinyal, benzin depo kapağı, yağ akıntısı, aracın altı (kedi-köpek) kontrol edilir.
Before getting in the car: tires, brake lamp, stop lamp, signals, fuel cap, oil leaks, under the car (cats & dogs) are checked.

Koltuk sırt açısı: yaklaşık 90°.
Seat back angle: approximately 90°.

Omuzlar koltuk sırtına yapışık olmalıdır.
Shoulders must stay in contact with the seat back.

Sol ayak, ayak dayama yerinde (dead pedal) vücudu koltuğa iter.
The left foot on the dead pedal pushes the body into the seat.

Koltuk ileri-geri ayarı: kol uzatıldığında bilek direksiyonun üst noktasında olmalıdır.
Seat forward/back adjustment: when the arm is extended, the wrist should rest on the top of the steering wheel.

Doğru oturma pozisyonu reaksiyon süresini KISALTIR (slide'daki "uzatır" ifadesi bir hatadır).
The correct seating position SHORTENS reaction time (the slide's "lengthens" is a typo).
